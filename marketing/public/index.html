For communities

Your community should still work when the network doesn't.

A tenants union carrying alerts, meeting changes, rides, and checklists. Disaster responders carrying shelter maps and supply requests into disconnected areas. A mutual-aid network coordinating needs, offers, schedules, and knowledge. An independent publication keeping and re-sharing its material when a website is blocked or a gateway disappears.

These are examples of what Riot is designed to enable — not claims that Riot has completed field validation for each kind of organization.

  • Keep working locally. Reading, writing, organizing, and preparing updates happen on the device — no server round-trip required.
  • Exchange without internet. Nearby devices can connect, review offered updates, and choose whether to add them. Current evidence is prototype local-network (Bonjour) testing; physical two-iPhone Bluetooth remains unverified.
  • Publish a public web view. The reference gateway renders exported public community content for ordinary browsers. It is not yet a live sync server for Riot devices.
  • Carry more than messages. A community can carry structured information and community-approved tools — checklists, wikis, events, decisions — not just a chat log.
  • Manage several communities. Creating, following, switching, archiving, restoring, and reopening communities are supported in the core; the Using Riot guide names which screens exist on each platform.
  • Keep human control. External updates are previewed before acceptance, and unapproved community tools do not execute.

Riot turns every participating device into part of the community's library, newsroom, toolbox, and distribution network.

For partners, funders, and journalists

Infrastructure communities can possess, not merely access.

A Riot community's Willow data carries its own continuity — it is not derived from a domain name or a hosted account. Participant devices provide shared custody of the community's information. Public gateways are replaceable views over exported public data, not the authority. Communities choose their tools and their editorial practices, and public Newswires can combine open publishing with transparent editorial actions instead of invisible ranking.

01

Community media

Publishing without one canonical publishing server — the readers' devices carry the material, and a blocked website is a lost window, not a lost archive.

02

Coordination that survives outages

Ordinary community infrastructure — boards, schedules, checklists — that remains locally useful during outages, shutdowns, or censorship.

03

Tools each community chooses

Organizers approve the tools their community carries. There is no central app store deciding for every community at once.

04

Shared foundations

The same substrate serves mutual aid, tenant organizing, clinics, cooperatives, disaster response, protests, and independent media.

This section makes no claim of production readiness, completed security audits, an availability guarantee, completed remote-device sync, or confidentiality for the public Newswire. See what works today.

For builders

Willow separates shared data from the network carrying it.

Riot publishes your update into the community's Willow space. Willow is designed so the update is not permanently tied to one transport. Riot's current prototype carries updates locally; internet seed synchronization is the next path under development.

Offline and online do not need separate community databases. They can be different ways of carrying the same shared state.

Under the hood

Willow provides

  • independent namespaces;
  • subspaces, paths, timestamps, and arbitrary payloads;
  • deterministic joins for partial replicas;
  • a data model independent of any one network transport; and
  • optional Meadowcap capability and Willow synchronization protocols.

Riot adds

  • community and profile semantics;
  • community relationships and per-community data identities;
  • public Newswire records and editorial actions;
  • community-approved, content-addressed mini-apps;
  • app-scoped bridge access and hardened native execution;
  • preview, validation, and acceptance policy;
  • durable multi-community management;
  • transport integrations and public export rendering; and
  • native interfaces for people who should never need to learn Willow.

Willow itself does not define Riot profiles, communities, Newswires, moderation, mini-apps, gateways, or native sandbox behavior — that is Riot's layer.

For the technically curious

Every post is a signed entry in a Willow-protocol namespace — content-addressed and capability-scoped, built for exactly this kind of partial, offline-first sync.

Device-to-device sync is one small, transport-independent exchange: Hello → each side sends a Summary of what it has → each side Requests only what it's missing → Entries cross the wire → both sides send an updated Summary → Complete. Malformed, oversized, or out-of-sequence messages get a hard Reject, not a best-effort parse. The same frame bytes travel over local IP, QR, or a plain file — the sync layer doesn't know or care which.

That exchange still can't skip the same admission boundary the UI uses: inspect → preview → plan → commit. Nothing is live in your store until you've explicitly planned and committed it — sync just gets bytes to your device, it doesn't get to decide what you publish.

Your signing key never leaves your device in the clear. It's sealed behind your device's hardware-backed keystore — Keychain on iOS, Android Keystore on Android — and only unwrapped in memory for the moment it's needed.

How it works

One story, wherever you meet Riot

Five beats hold the whole mental model — what Riot actually verifies, and what a browser mirror could lie about. Read the web for reach; open the app when provenance matters. The screen-by-screen workflow follows below.

  1. No central account or publishing server

    Your identity is a cryptographic key, not a service login. Volunteer seeds, anchors, and mirrors can run on servers, but none owns your identity or is the single place Riot must publish.

  2. Publishing moves peer to peer

    Signed posts move between phones and volunteer seeds. Peer-to-peer does not mean anonymous: devices and infrastructure may observe connections.

  3. Many mirrors, not one site

    Websites are replaceable views, not the authority. A mirror can display altered text or false attribution, but it cannot produce an independently synced signed record Riot accepts as the claimed author.

  4. Signed records, checked in the app

    Riot checks the signature and authorization of the independently synced record. That establishes who signed an unchanged admitted record—not whether its claims are true, current, complete, safe, or endorsed.

  5. Web for reach; the app for provenance

    Use the web to reach readers. When provenance matters, read the independently synced record in Riot instead of trusting what a mirror displayed.

What you do

What actually happens, screen by screen

01

Preload

Install it before you need it. With data already held on a functioning device, local tools can remain useful when no internet path is available.

02

Open or create a community

Create a public community with a name — "Berlin Mutual Aid" — or join one that already exists with a link or QR code. A community is a namespace; there's no admin approval gating who can publish into it.

03

Compose & sign

Draft a headline and what people need to know. Flag it honestly if a model helped write it. Nothing goes anywhere until you tap "review complete — sign locally" — a model can draft, it can't sign, and it can't publish.

04

Carry it

A signed entry is just bytes — hand it off any way a bytestring can move: a file or QR code, or the prototype's tested local-network exchange between nearby devices. Physical two-iPhone Bluetooth remains unverified.

05

Preview before you accept

Incoming entries land in an import queue first. You see the signer and the content before anything touches your board — nothing is accepted automatically, ever.

06

Merge, don't overwrite

When a compatible exchange path is available, devices can merge records they have received. Riot cannot recover records that no reachable participant holds.

Privacy

Privacy through control, not secrecy.

Riot is privacy-respecting, not secret by default. Public community updates are meant to circulate. Privacy comes from reducing centralized collection, keeping community data on participants' devices, supporting separate community identities, limiting what tools can access, and letting people exchange information without always exposing their activity to internet infrastructure.

Riot cannot promise anonymity, conceal public posts, or erase every copy after information has spread. Encrypted private groups are planned separately and are not shipped today.

You control your participation and your local data — not every copy of public information once it has been shared.

What Riot does not hide

  • public Newswire content;
  • IP addresses or ordinary metadata visible to any internet service used;
  • nearby radio presence, device labels, proximity, and timing;
  • copies already carried away by another device, export, backup, or log; and
  • identity correlations from reused names, writing style, behavior, timing, proximity, or network metadata.

Separate per-community keys give pseudonym separation at the data layer — pseudonymity is not anonymity. And a web gateway is a presentation a browser reader chooses to trust: it can show a view that is censored, stale, incomplete, fabricated, or incorrectly rendered, and it can log connection metadata like any website. Riot clients validate community data independently; browsers don't.

Open Newswire

Public by design

Open publishing for alerts, mutual aid, reporting, and community publications — meant to circulate, plaintext, with transparent editorial actions as a lens rather than a gate.

Encrypted private groups

Planned

Private, encrypted spaces for affinity groups, coops, and crews are a separate planned system with its own threat model. They are not part of today's prototype.

Not guesswork

Grounded in the field, not assumed at a whiteboard

Every major design call is checked against how mutual aid networks, protest comms, and movement media have actually worked — Occupy Sandy, TXTMob, Verificado 19S, Indymedia's RNC desk, the 2019 Hong Kong protests, and disaster response after the 2023 Türkiye earthquakes and Hurricane Maria — not assumption.

Each research pass runs multiple independent search angles, then puts every extracted claim in front of three adversarial reviewers whose default is to try to refute it. Only what survives makes it into the design.

Status

Built in the open

Riot is a prototype being built right now, in public — every increment lands on the releases page as it ships. Every claim above carries its own label; this is the summary, not a correction.

Working in the prototype

  • Durable core support for creating, following, switching, archiving, restoring, and reopening multiple communities
  • Native iOS community chooser and link/QR join flows
  • Local Newswire creation and durable display
  • Human confirmation and preview-before-accept import in nearby flows
  • Tested local-network (Bonjour) peer exchange
  • Public gateway rendering from exported community data
  • Community-approved mini-app packages
  • App-scoped bridge access and hardened app execution
  • Fresh per-community author identities when joining another community

Direction being built or still unverified

  • Encrypted private groups
  • Confidentiality for public communities
  • Full deletion from devices that already copied public content
  • Integrated remote Riot-device synchronization through an internet seed
  • Production scale, audited security, or an availability guarantee
  • Full interoperability with every Willow draft and transport
  • Physical two-iPhone Bluetooth exchange
